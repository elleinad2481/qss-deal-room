The QSS Deal Room

At QSS, a client opportunity isn't evaluated with a research summary — it's evaluated with a full case. Operations logic. A prototype. A data plan. A financial argument. Your team builds all of it, drawing on everything you've covered in class.

The Central Question

Everything your team produces — every slide, every number, every recommendation — should help QSS leadership answer one question:

"Is this client a smart engagement for QSS — operationally, analytically, and financially — and what evidence supports that judgment?"

How This Works

Your team picks one client from five options. Using the concepts and skills you've already covered in class, you build a complete feasibility case — four phases, three deliverables, one recommendation.

Assignment Structure

The Four Phases

Each phase connects directly to content you've already covered in class — the Business Model Canvas, Infrastructure & Logistics, Cost Structure, and Validation. Your deck must address all four in order, building from operational logic to financial recommendation.

The consulting sequence
Infrastructure & Logistics → Prototype & Validate → Validation Methods → Cost Structure → Decision
Phase 1 · Infrastructure, Logistics & the Business Model Canvas
Operational Logic & Cost Drivers

Remember from class: infrastructure and logistics provide a solid and reliable formula that ensures consistency and can lead to scalability — without a plan, an increase in business can lead to failure. This phase asks you to apply that thinking directly to your client. Use the Business Model Canvas as your framework.

Course concepts to apply here
Key Activities Key Resources Key Partnerships 7Rs of Logistics Infrastructure Components Supply Chain

Your slides for this phase must cover:

Key Activities map — using the Business Model Canvas, identify the things QSS must do every day to execute this engagement successfully (e.g., discovery, analysis, stakeholder interviews, prototype build, reporting). These are not vague tasks — they are specific, named activities with a verb.
Key Resources — what does QSS need to own or control to deliver? Identify physical resources (laptops, office space), intellectual resources (data access, client knowledge), human resources (which roles on the team), and financial resources (budget, tools, software subscriptions).
Key Partnerships — who outside of QSS would this project require working with? This could be client-side teams, data vendors, subject matter experts, or implementation partners. Describe what each partner contributes and why QSS can't do it alone.
Infrastructure needs — what computing, IT services, facilities, or transport would this engagement require? Think beyond "a laptop" — consider data pipelines, software platforms, security tools, and any physical infrastructure your client needs to operate.
Logistics check — apply the 7Rs of Logistics to your client's delivery challenge: are they getting the right product to the right customer at the right price, in the right quantity, in the right condition, at the right time, in the right place? Where is the 7Rs chain breaking down for them?
Execution failure points — at least two specific ways this project could break down, and your mitigation plan for each. "Something might go wrong" is not a failure point — name the exact moment and reason.
Goal: Show that you understand how business activities translate into resources, costs, and operational risk — using the same Business Model Canvas framework you've been building with in class.
Phase 2 · Validation — Steps & Assumptions
Prototype & Assumption Testing

From class: validation is the process of determining if there is a need for an idea or product — a useful tool that reduces the cost and risk of production while speeding up delivery and launch. Before QSS commits to a full engagement, it needs to test its most critical assumptions first.

Course concepts to apply here
Steps of Validation Assumptions Prototyping Pivot Logic Goals of Validation

Your slides for this phase must include:

Your written assumptions — state 3–5 assumptions your team is making about this client and their situation. Remember from class: assumptions are aspects of your idea that, if invalidated, would require you to pivot. Format them clearly: "We assume that [X] is true because [Y]." Test your most influential ones first.
A draft prototype — what would QSS actually deliver for this client? Show a rough version: a mock dashboard layout, a sample report outline, a process map wireframe, a decision framework, or a workflow sketch. It does not need to be polished — it needs to be real and testable.
What success looks like — from your validation notes: determine what success will be defined as for you. Before gathering any feedback, define the benchmark. If you show this prototype to 10 people, what response would tell you it's working?
Pivot logic — if your customer segment rejects part of your idea, what changes to your approach would you make to meet their needs? Map out at least one "if this assumption fails, we do this instead" scenario.
Iteration plan — who gives feedback on the prototype, how often, and what "improved" looks like after the first cycle.
Goal: Show that your team has followed the validation steps from class: you've written your goals and assumptions, defined what success means, and built something you can actually test — before QSS commits to the full engagement.
Phase 3 · Validation Methods & Data Collection
Validation Plan & Data Strategy

From class: the goal of validation is to find out what your customer segment is willing to buy and for how much — it is not to argue, and you should not try to lead them to a conclusion. This phase asks you to design the specific methods QSS would use to gather the evidence that either confirms or challenges your assumptions.

Course concepts to apply here
Primary Forms of Validation Question Styles Likert Scale Open-Ended Questions Demographic Questions Customer Segment Research

Your slides for this phase must include:

Which primary forms of validation you'd use — talking to customers directly, talking to experts, surveying, or research. For each one you choose, explain why it's the right tool for your specific client and assumption. Not every engagement needs all four.
Survey design (if applicable) — if your validation plan includes a survey, identify the question styles you'd use and why. From class, you know the tradeoffs: Likert scales measure attitudes but respondents can click through; open-ended questions enable rich responses but are harder to analyze; multiple choice is clean but limits what you can learn. Make the right choice for what you're trying to discover.
Who you're validating with — your customer segment matters. Describe exactly who you'd talk to, survey, or research. Use demographic thinking: what characteristics matter most for this client's problem (industry, role, age, geography, company size)?
Data → decision flow — a process flow (visual or bullet-format) showing which specific data point or survey result would trigger which specific decision. Not "we would analyze it," but: if the Likert score on X is below 3.5, we recommend Y. If more than 60% of respondents say Z, we pivot.
Market size check — from validation steps in class: assess your market size. How many people/organizations fall into your client's target customer segment? Use this to determine whether the problem is big enough to justify QSS's engagement.
Goal: Show that you know how to gather real evidence — not just describe data you'd want, but design the method, the questions, and the decision logic that turns responses into a clear recommendation.
Phase 4 · Cost Structure & Financial Reasoning
Financial Logic & Recommendation

From class: the cost structure is a collection of business expenses that encompasses all the money needed to operate the business — this is more than just the cost to make a good or service. This phase asks you to apply cost structure thinking to your client and to QSS, and turn it into a financially supported recommendation.

Course concepts to apply here
Fixed vs Variable Costs Cost-Driven vs Value-Driven Economies of Scale Cost Allocation Value-Based vs Cost-Based Pricing Revenue Streams

Your slides for this phase must include:

Cost structure classification — identify your client's major fixed costs (expenses that stay the same regardless of business volume — like rent, salaried staff, and software subscriptions) and variable costs (expenses that change with activity — like materials, commissions, or per-unit delivery costs). This distinction matters because it tells you how the business scales.
Cost-driven vs. value-driven diagnosis — is your client's business model primarily cost-driven (competing on minimizing price through automation and efficiency) or value-driven (competing on quality, experience, or brand)? How does this affect what QSS should recommend and how it should price its own engagement?
Basic cost-benefit analysis — what does this engagement cost QSS to deliver? What value does it create for the client? Show the math with clearly labeled assumptions. Every number must be traceable back to a source or stated assumption.
Economies of scale consideration — does your client's problem get better or worse as they grow? Where would an economies-of-scale effect kick in — meaning, where do their per-unit costs drop as volume increases? This matters for understanding whether QSS's solution has lasting value.
Three-case sensitivity — best case, base case, worst case. Show what numbers change between scenarios and why. The worst case in particular should be grounded: what realistic set of conditions would lead to the engagement failing to pay back?
Recommendation with financial reason — Proceed / Proceed With Conditions / Do Not Proceed. Support it with one financial reason specific enough that a QSS executive could repeat it without the slides.
Goal: Show that you understand the cost structure concepts from class — fixed vs. variable, cost-driven vs. value-driven, and cost allocation — and can apply them to make a financially grounded recommendation, not just a gut feeling dressed up in numbers.
What to Submit

Three Deliverables

Your team submits three connected pieces that work as a package. They're designed to reinforce each other — the one-pager forces synthesis, the memo forces rigor, the deck forces clarity.

1
The One-Pager — Visual Snapshot
A single-page visual document that gives a QSS executive a complete picture of the opportunity in under 60 seconds. Think of this as your "leave-behind." It does not repeat your slides — it distills them. This is a design challenge as much as a content challenge.
Client name, what they do, and the core problem QSS is solving — 2–3 sentences max
A simple operations or process flow showing how QSS would execute the work
A cost/benefit snapshot — your key numbers, with labeled assumptions
Your recommendation (Proceed / Proceed With Conditions / Do Not Proceed) with one supporting reason
Format: PDF or designed document. Infographic layout strongly encouraged. This is not a paragraph summary — it should communicate visually.
2
The Slide Deck — Your Full Analysis
An 8–10 slide deck that walks through your team's complete analysis across all four phases. Each section of the deck corresponds to one phase. Opening and closing framing is expected.
Slide 1 — The Opportunity: Who is the client, what is the problem, and why does it matter right now?
Slides 2–3 — Operations & Cost Logic (Phase 1): Workstreams, resource plan, cost drivers, failure points and mitigations
Slides 4–5 — The Prototype (Phase 2): Draft artifact, assumptions being tested, iteration plan
Slides 6–7 — Data & Validation Plan (Phase 3): Specific data needed, collection method, process flow, decisions enabled
Slides 8–9 — Financial Logic & Recommendation (Phase 4): Cost-benefit analysis, ROI/payback metric, 3-case sensitivity, recommendation with financial reason
Slide 10 — Risk & Open Questions: 2–3 things that could make your recommendation wrong
Format: PDF or PowerPoint. Professional, clean design. This is an internal QSS deck — not a class project, not a school report.
3
The Supporting Memo — Show Your Work
A 1–2 page internal QSS memo submitted alongside the deck. This is not a summary of your slides — it goes deeper. It's where you show the math, the sources, and the reasoning that couldn't fit in a slide.
The full financial calculation for your cost-benefit analysis, with every assumption labeled and traceable
Your three-case sensitivity table (best / base / worst) with the specific numbers that drive each scenario
A citation list — minimum 3 credible external sources, including at least one financial or industry source
Any additional data, research, or reasoning that supports your recommendation but didn't fit the deck
Format: Word document or PDF. Internal QSS memo header. Professional tone. 1–2 pages only.

Company Background

Eden Home Solutions was founded six years ago by two electrical engineers who were convinced that home automation was too complicated and too expensive for the average renter. Their first product — a wireless hub that connected to existing light switches, thermostats, and door locks without rewiring — became a breakout hit in dense urban apartment buildings. Property managers loved it because no construction was required. Tenants loved it because it worked reliably and didn't require a $3,000 installation fee.

Eden grew almost entirely through B2B partnerships with apartment building operators. A property signs an enterprise deal, Eden installs the hub in common areas and individual units, and tenants access everything through a clean mobile app. The revenue model is a combination of a one-time hardware kit fee per unit and a monthly subscription for the software layer ($8–12/month per unit, depending on contract size). At their peak urban density, margins were strong and churn was low.

Two years ago, Eden raised a $22M Series B round with one primary thesis: that the same product could work in suburban single-family homes. The suburban homeowner market is ten times larger. The path seemed obvious.

The Problem

The suburban expansion has not gone according to plan. Eden has signed deals with three regional homebuilder partnerships and launched a direct-to-consumer channel for existing homeowners, but the numbers are significantly behind projections eighteen months into the expansion.

Urban apartment deals closed at an average of 340 units per contract. Suburban homebuilder deals average 18 units per quarter per builder — and the direct-to-consumer channel is acquiring customers at a cost of $210 per household, nearly four times the enterprise cost. The monthly subscription churn rate in suburban markets is 8.4% — compared to 1.9% in urban apartment markets.

Leadership's instinct is that the product needs to be simplified for homeowners who don't have an on-site property manager to help with setup. The product team thinks the issue is pricing — suburban homeowners compare Eden to off-the-shelf smart home hardware from big box retailers and balk at the subscription. The sales team thinks the channel strategy is wrong and that Eden needs local installation partners instead of trying to sell direct.

Nobody has done the analysis to know which of these is right. That's the ask to QSS.

Financial Situation

Eden is 14 months from its current cash runway at current burn. The suburban expansion is burning $680K/month in sales, marketing, and operational overhead — and the segment is not yet profitable. The urban business remains healthy and cash-generative, but the board has made clear that the next financing round will require demonstrated suburban traction.

Eden's leadership has set a threshold: if the suburban segment cannot reach breakeven unit economics (i.e., monthly subscription revenue covering customer acquisition cost within 12 months) within the next fiscal year, they will consider exiting the consumer channel and focusing on B2B homebuilder-only.

What Eden Wants From QSS
→
Diagnose the suburban churn problem. Is it product complexity? Pricing? Wrong customer segment? Wrong channel? Eden needs to know what's actually driving the 8.4% monthly churn before they spend another dollar trying to fix it.
→
Model the economics of each growth path. Enterprise homebuilder deals vs. direct-to-consumer vs. a local installer partner network — what are the unit economics, payback periods, and capital requirements for each path?
→
Recommend a focused suburban strategy. With 14 months of runway, Eden cannot pursue all three paths simultaneously. QSS should help leadership pick one, resource it properly, and define the metrics that would prove it's working.
Key questions your team must address

What data would you need to distinguish between a product problem, a pricing problem, and a channel problem? How would you prototype a recommendation before Eden commits resources to it? What does "breakeven unit economics within 12 months" actually translate to in terms of churn rate, CAC, and LTV — and which of Eden's three paths gets there first?

Organization Background

Harborview Health Network is a regional nonprofit hospital system operating four campuses across two counties. Founded in 1961 as a community hospital serving a mid-sized port city, Harborview has grown through two decades of acquisitions into a full-spectrum network offering emergency, surgical, primary care, and behavioral health services. They employ approximately 2,800 clinical and administrative staff and serve roughly 94,000 unique patients per year.

Harborview is not in financial distress — their operating margin is thin but stable at approximately 1.8%. However, they are facing increasing pressure from two directions: the federal CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services) Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program, which penalizes hospitals financially for excess readmissions in specific diagnostic categories, and a competitive threat from a regional for-profit hospital chain that opened a new facility 11 miles from Harborview's largest campus eighteen months ago.

Their CMO (Chief Medical Officer) and COO have jointly agreed that improving post-discharge care coordination is their single highest-priority operational initiative for the coming fiscal year. They hired an analytics coordinator eight months ago specifically to address this. She has built a comprehensive discharge dataset going back 26 months — and has had almost no time to actually analyze it because she has been consumed by reporting requests from clinical leadership.

The Problem

Harborview's 30-day readmission rate of 18.4% is 3.1 percentage points above the national average for hospital systems of their size and payer mix. CMS has flagged them for excess readmissions in three diagnostic categories: heart failure, pneumonia, and hip/knee replacement. The estimated annual CMS penalty exposure from these flags is approximately $2.1 million — a meaningful number for a system operating at 1.8% margin.

Beyond the financial penalty, clinical leadership believes that many of these readmissions are preventable. Post-discharge follow-up is inconsistent: some patients receive a nurse phone call within 48 hours, some receive a follow-up appointment, and some receive nothing until they show up in the emergency department. The process is not standardized, and there is no systematic way to identify which discharged patients are at highest risk of readmission before something goes wrong.

The analytics coordinator has identified that the discharge dataset contains enough information to build a basic readmission risk score — but she hasn't been given the resources, time, or organizational authority to implement anything that would change clinical workflows. Her analysis has been presented to the CMO twice in the form of internal memos that have not resulted in action.

Financial Situation

Each percentage point reduction in readmission rate across Harborview's system translates to approximately 940 fewer readmissions per year, based on their patient volume and current rate. The fully-loaded cost of an avoidable readmission — including direct clinical costs, bed utilization, and CMS penalty risk — is estimated internally at $8,200 per event. A 2-point reduction in readmission rate would generate approximately $15.4M in cost avoidance and penalty reduction annually.

The CMO has a discretionary budget of $800K for care coordination initiatives this fiscal year. She is willing to allocate up to $350K to an analytics and workflow implementation project if there is a credible business case.

What Harborview Wants From QSS
→
Build a readmission risk stratification framework. Using the existing discharge dataset, QSS should design a model (or model outline) that identifies which discharged patients are at high, medium, and low risk of readmission within 30 days — without requiring new data collection.
→
Design a tiered post-discharge intervention protocol. Different risk levels warrant different interventions. A high-risk heart failure patient should receive something different from a low-risk hip replacement patient. QSS should prototype what a tiered protocol looks like operationally — not clinically design it, but map the workflow.
→
Quantify the financial case for the CMO. The $350K budget decision requires a clear financial argument. QSS should model the expected ROI of a readmission reduction initiative — with realistic assumptions about what a 1–2 point reduction would require and what it would return.
Key questions your team must address

What variables in a discharge dataset are most predictive of readmission risk, and how would you turn those into a workable risk score for clinical staff? What does the prototype of a "tiered intervention protocol" look like — and how would you test whether it changes behavior? How do you translate a readmission rate reduction into a dollar figure that justifies a $350K investment?

Company Background

GreenLoop Apparel is a four-year-old sustainable fashion brand that designs, manufactures, and sells a line of everyday basics — t-shirts, hoodies, joggers, and outerwear — made entirely from certified recycled and organic materials. The founders came from the conventional apparel industry and were deliberately building an alternative: a brand that could be transparent about its supply chain, certifiable under third-party environmental standards (they hold B-Corp and GOTS certifications), and still priced accessibly enough for mainstream consumers.

For the first two years, GreenLoop operated as a pure direct-to-consumer brand, selling exclusively through their own website and two seasonal pop-up markets. Growth was steady but modest. The inflection point came when a regional outdoor retailer placed a wholesale order for 1,200 units of their recycled-cotton hoodie. The retailer sold through in six weeks and reordered. Three more wholesale accounts followed within four months. The B2B wholesale channel has grown from zero to 34% of revenue in 18 months — and it is growing faster than the DTC business.

This is the good news. The bad news is that GreenLoop's operations were built for a DTC business, not a wholesale one. And the differences are significant.

The Problem

GreenLoop's supply chain is built around small-batch, made-to-order production with a 14–18 week lead time from their two certified manufacturers — one in Portugal and one in Vietnam. For DTC, this worked because they could plan production 4 months ahead based on modest, predictable demand. For wholesale, it doesn't work — retailers need confirmed inventory commitments 6–8 months in advance, and they order in volumes that GreenLoop often can't guarantee without committing to production before knowing the retailer's final order size.

The result: GreenLoop has experienced four significant stockout events in the past 12 months — situations where they received confirmed wholesale orders they could not fulfill at full volume. In three of those cases, the retailer accepted a partial fulfillment. In one case, the retailer cancelled the order and has not reordered. Additionally, GreenLoop has ended up with significant overstock positions on two SKUs (a particular colorway of their signature jogger and their lightweight rain shell) — inventory that has been discounted 35% to move and has compressed margins on those items.

The operations director has been managing all of this manually — tracking purchase orders in spreadsheets, production timelines in a shared Google Doc, and retailer commitments in email threads. There is no system. There is no forecast. There is no early warning when a wholesale account's order timeline doesn't match a production lead time.

Financial Situation

GreenLoop's blended gross margin is 52% — strong for the industry. But the stockout losses (estimated at 19% of potential wholesale revenue in the last fiscal year) and the overstock writedowns ($142K in the past year) are real cash drains. If the stockout problem were solved and overstock reduced by 50%, the company estimates it would recover approximately $380K in annual contribution margin — more than the full salary cost of an operations analyst.

The founders are currently bootstrapped and are beginning to explore a small raise ($1.5–2M) to fund inventory and operations improvements. Any investment in operations needs to have a clear financial story: what does it cost, what does it return, and when does it pay back?

What GreenLoop Wants From QSS
→
Map and diagnose the current operations flow. Before recommending solutions, QSS should document what actually happens today — from retailer order inquiry to production commitment to delivery — and identify the specific points where mismatches occur between demand signals and supply capacity.
→
Design a minimum viable forecasting and inventory system. GreenLoop doesn't need enterprise software — they need a system that is simple enough to actually use with a 6-person ops team. QSS should prototype what a basic demand forecasting and inventory tracking workflow would look like for a company of their size.
→
Build the financial case for the operations investment. The founders need a clear answer to: "What does it cost to fix this, and what does fixing it return?" QSS should model the cost of the operations improvement against the revenue and margin recovery from reduced stockouts and overstock.
Key questions your team must address

How do you diagnose which part of an operations flow is creating a mismatch — the demand side (forecasting), the supply side (production commitments), or the communication layer between them? What does a "minimum viable" operations system look like for a 6-person team — and what assumptions does that require about their capacity to adopt new tools? How do you convert stockout and overstock losses into a financial model that justifies an operations investment?

Company Background

Apex Performance Sports Group operates seven premium athletic training facilities across a metropolitan region. They are not a traditional gym — they position themselves as performance training centers, staffed by certified strength coaches and athletic trainers rather than personal trainers. Their members skew toward serious recreational athletes: competitive amateur cyclists, triathletes, adult rec-league sports participants, and people recovering from injuries who want structured athletic rehabilitation.

Membership is structured as an annual commitment at $400/month (Apex Premier) or a 6-month commitment at $450/month (Apex Flex). There are no month-to-month memberships — Apex believes the commitment structure is part of what makes their members take their training seriously. The model has worked: Apex has 3,200 active members across seven locations and generates approximately $15.4M in annual membership revenue, supplemented by $2.1M in personal coaching packages and equipment sales.

Three years ago, Apex was growing at 22% annually and had plans to open four more locations. Today, growth has stalled at approximately 6% — and the leadership team is convinced that the attrition problem is the root cause. They are right.

The Problem

Apex's 31% annual attrition rate is extremely high for a premium, commitment-based fitness brand. Industry benchmarks for performance-oriented facilities with annual memberships typically run 18–22%. At Apex's member base and price point, the gap between their 31% attrition and a 20% benchmark represents approximately $1.7M in annual revenue — members who should have renewed but didn't.

The pattern of attrition is particularly troubling. Across all seven locations, members who cancel do so predominantly in three windows: during months 2–3 (early adopters who don't integrate the habit), around month 7–8 (mid-contract disengagement), and at contract renewal (month 12 or month 6). Exit surveys exist but have a 14% response rate, and the responses skew toward price complaints even when the data team suspects that utilization — how often members actually show up — is a stronger predictor.

Each of Apex's seven locations has its own general manager, its own coaching staff culture, and, functionally, its own unofficial policies. Two locations have significantly lower attrition than the others (19% and 21%), but no one has done a systematic analysis of what those locations are doing differently. The corporate team has anecdotal theories — one GM does monthly check-in calls with members who haven't been in for 10+ days; another offers a structured 90-day onboarding program — but nothing has been measured or replicated.

Financial Situation

Apex's unit economics are strong when members stay. The fully-loaded cost to acquire a new member (marketing, trial, onboarding) runs approximately $620 per member. The lifetime value of a retained member who renews twice (3 years) is approximately $14,400 — an LTV:CAC ratio of 23:1. The lifetime value of a member who churns at month 8 is approximately $3,200 — an LTV:CAC ratio of 5:1. The difference is almost entirely in whether the retention interventions work.

Apex's CEO has approved a retention initiative budget of $280K for the current fiscal year. She has been explicit: the money should go toward analytics and system improvements, not discounts or promotions. "We are not going to buy our way out of an attrition problem," she said in the kick-off meeting with their ops team. "We need to understand why people leave and fix that."

What Apex Wants From QSS
→
Identify the leading indicators of churn. Using Apex's existing check-in and membership data, QSS should design an analysis framework that identifies which member behaviors — visit frequency, session types, coach relationships, time of day patterns — most strongly predict early cancellation. The goal is an early warning system, not a post-mortem.
→
Analyze and codify what the high-retention locations are doing. The two low-attrition locations have answers that Apex needs. QSS should design a framework for extracting those answers — through interviews, process documentation, or data comparison — and translating them into replicable operational standards.
→
Build the financial model for the retention initiative. What does a 5-point reduction in annual attrition rate (from 31% to 26%) translate to in annual revenue and LTV? What would it cost to implement the interventions? What is the ROI, and over what time period does it pay back?
Key questions your team must address

What data would you need to build a churn early warning system — and how far in advance of cancellation would you want to be able to predict it? How do you study what two locations are doing well and turn that into a replicable system for seven? And what does the math look like when you translate attrition reduction into LTV — specifically, how does the 31% → 26% scenario change the financial story for Apex?

Company Background

Northshore Property Group is a privately held residential property management firm managing approximately 4,800 units across 22 apartment communities in a mid-sized metropolitan area. They do not own the properties — they manage them on behalf of institutional and individual real estate investors, charging a management fee of 8–10% of collected rent. The business model is fundamentally one of operational scale: the more units you manage well, the more fees you generate, and the more stable your revenue base.

Northshore was founded 14 years ago by a commercial real estate attorney who recognized that most small-to-midsize apartment owners had no operational infrastructure. She built the company by acquiring management contracts from local owners who wanted professional management but couldn't afford to build their own teams. Today, Northshore employs 44 people — 12 at corporate and 32 in the field across their 22 properties — and manages approximately $104M in annual rent collections.

Two years ago, Northshore implemented a new property management software platform that captures lease data, maintenance requests, renewal outcomes, vacancy timelines, and payment history at the unit level. Before that, most of this data lived in paper files and individual Excel sheets at each property. The new system is comprehensive. It has been running for 26 months. Nobody at Northshore has done any meaningful analysis of what's in it.

The Problem

Over the past 18 months, Northshore has seen three metrics drift in the wrong direction simultaneously: occupancy has declined from a portfolio average of 94.2% to 91.4%, average days-vacant per unit turn has increased from 18 days to 27 days, and the renewal rate has dropped from 61% to 54%. No single cause has been identified. Leadership's current theory is that the market has softened — but comparable properties in the same submarkets are not showing the same deterioration.

The operations director has flagged what she calls "invisible churn" — tenants who don't formally complain but also don't renew, and leave no trail of reasons. Maintenance request data shows that several properties have above-average open ticket rates (requests submitted but not resolved within 72 hours), but the connection between maintenance responsiveness and renewal behavior has never been analyzed. Similarly, asking rents across the portfolio have been set based on "what the market will bear" as estimated by individual property managers — there is no systematic approach to pricing that accounts for local market data, unit characteristics, or historical performance by floor, facing, or unit size.

The CEO's concern is simple and direct: "If we lose a management contract because a property owner looks at our renewal rates and decides they can do better somewhere else, we've lost not just the management fee — we've lost the relationship, and those owners talk to each other."

Financial Situation

Northshore's fee revenue is $8.3M annually (8–10% of $104M in rent collected). Each percentage point increase in portfolio occupancy translates to approximately $1.04M in additional rent collected — and $83–104K in additional management fees to Northshore. Each day reduction in average vacancy per turn, across 4,800 units with a 38% turnover rate, saves approximately $128,400 in annual vacancy loss (to property owners) and reduces risk of contract loss. A 3-point improvement in renewal rate (from 54% to 57%) would prevent approximately 144 additional turnovers per year at an average turnover cost of $3,800.

Northshore's CEO is willing to invest up to $200K in an analytics-driven operations improvement initiative. She views it partly as an investment in client retention (property owners who see improved metrics don't switch managers) and partly as a competitive differentiator in new contract pitches.

What Northshore Wants From QSS
→
Diagnose the renewal rate decline. Using the 26 months of data in the property management platform, QSS should identify the most likely drivers of declining renewal rates — maintenance responsiveness, pricing variance, unit characteristics, lease timing, or something else — and quantify which properties and which tenant segments are driving the problem.
→
Design a market-based pricing discipline framework. Northshore needs a systematic approach to setting and adjusting asking rents that is based on public market data and internal performance — not individual property manager intuition. QSS should prototype what a pricing review process would look like, including guardrails that prevent overpricing vacancies in weak micromarkets.
→
Model the NOI impact of operations improvements. The CEO needs to make a $200K investment decision. QSS should model the impact of a combined occupancy, renewal, and vacancy-day improvement initiative — with realistic assumptions about what 12–24 months of focused operations work could achieve — and calculate the return to both Northshore and the property owners they serve.
Key questions your team must address

How would you use maintenance request data to test the hypothesis that responsiveness predicts renewals? What does a "market-based pricing framework" look like for a property manager — specifically, what data inputs, what decision rules, and what limits? And how do you model the financial impact of operational improvements for both Northshore (management fee revenue) and the property owners (NOI) simultaneously?

Fresh Route is a three-year-old grocery delivery service that serves senior communities — specifically, residents of independent-living facilities and nearby neighborhoods who have difficulty getting to stores. Their model: subscribers pay $18/month plus an 8% service fee on each order. A rotating team of 14 part-time drivers handles same-day delivery from two partnered grocery chains. The business works in Columbus, OH. The founders want to expand to two additional cities (Cleveland and Cincinnati) but have no data on whether the same model will work, no documented operations, and no clear picture of what expansion will actually cost.

1 Phase 1 · Infrastructure, Logistics & Business Model Canvas
Key Activities (Business Model Canvas)
Fresh Route's key activities in a new city would be: driver recruitment and onboarding (ongoing, variable to demand), grocery partner negotiation (one-time but critical), order processing and routing (daily), subscriber acquisition and marketing (ongoing), and customer service and issue resolution (daily). QSS's key activities for this engagement would be: market viability analysis, operations documentation, cost modeling, and launch readiness assessment.
Key Resources
Physical
Delivery vehicles (driver-owned), insulated bags, mobile devices for order management, a local dispatch hub or staging area in each new city
Intellectual
Order management app, grocery partner pricing agreements, subscriber data and behavior history, Columbus operational playbook (currently undocumented — this is a gap)
Human
14 part-time drivers (Columbus), 1 ops manager, 1 customer service rep. New cities would each need 8–12 drivers, 1 city manager before launch
Financial
~$85K estimated launch capital per new market (driver signing bonuses, local marketing, hub setup, 3 months of fixed costs before subscriber revenue covers them)
Key Partnerships
Local grocery chain partners are the single most critical partnership — without a signed agreement, Fresh Route cannot operate. In Cleveland and Cincinnati, no such agreements exist yet. Additional partnerships needed: a last-mile routing software vendor (currently using a basic tool that won't scale), senior living community referral programs, and potentially a regional food bank for surplus handling.
7Rs of Logistics — Where the Chain is Breaking
Fresh Route scores well on Right Product (seniors get what they ordered) and Right Customer (targeting is clear), but has problems at Right Quantity (orders sometimes arrive short due to grocery stockouts, with no protocol for handling substitutions), Right Time (same-day windows are missed ~11% of deliveries in Columbus during peak months), and Right Place (routing software causes inefficiencies when two drivers serve overlapping zones). These are the operational failures that would be amplified in two new cities if not addressed before expansion.
Execution Failure Points
Failure point 1 — No documented operations playbook. Fresh Route's Columbus model runs on informal knowledge held by two founders. If QSS attempts to replicate it in Cleveland without documenting it first, the new city will make the same early mistakes. Mitigation: QSS's first deliverable is a 2-week operations documentation sprint before any expansion planning begins.
Failure point 2 — Grocery partner not secured before launch commitment. If Fresh Route commits to a launch date before signing a local grocery partner, they will have acquired subscribers they cannot serve. Mitigation: QSS recommends a hard gate — no launch marketing until at least one grocery agreement is signed in each new city.
2 Phase 2 · Validation — Steps & Assumptions
Written Assumptions (test the most influential ones first)
Assumption 1 (most influential): We assume that seniors in Cleveland and Cincinnati face the same grocery access problem as seniors in Columbus. If this is wrong — if they have other solutions (family support, shuttle services) — the whole expansion thesis fails. Test first.
Assumption 2: We assume subscribers in new markets will pay $18/month + 8% fee. This might be wrong — income levels in Cleveland's senior population are lower than Columbus. Price sensitivity may require a different structure.
Assumption 3: We assume the Columbus operations model (driver counts, delivery windows, routing zones) is directly replicable. This may be wrong if city geography differs significantly.
Assumption 4: We assume local grocery chains in new markets will partner on similar pricing terms. A less favorable wholesale agreement would make unit economics unworkable.
Draft Prototype — New Market Onboarding Dashboard
The prototype QSS would build and test is a simple "new market readiness dashboard" — a one-page visual that a city manager would use to track the 12 prerequisites before a market launch (grocery partner signed, drivers recruited, routing zones mapped, etc.). It would be built first as a paper checklist, shown to the Columbus ops manager to confirm it captures the real launch sequence, then converted into a simple shared Google Sheet before becoming a tool in any launch software.
What success looks like for this prototype: the Columbus ops manager reviews the checklist and says "yes, if we'd had this before our own launch, we would have avoided at least 3 of our early problems." That's the validation threshold.
Pivot Logic
If Assumption 1 fails (seniors have other solutions and don't see the problem), the pivot is not to abandon grocery delivery but to target a different customer segment — not senior living facilities, but working adults in food deserts. If Assumption 2 fails (price is too high), the pivot is a lower-cost basic tier ($12/month, smaller delivery window, 3 orders/month cap) tested in one neighborhood before committing to a full market launch.
3 Phase 3 · Validation Methods & Data Collection
Primary Forms of Validation Selected
Talking to customers directly ✓
20 in-person interviews at two senior living facilities in Cleveland. Goal: test Assumption 1. Are they experiencing the problem? How are they currently solving it?
Talking to experts ✓
3 interviews with directors of senior services departments in Cleveland and Cincinnati. They know their populations' needs better than anyone and can flag whether similar services have failed before.
Surveying ✓
40-person survey distributed through senior centers. Used to test price sensitivity and baseline behavior at scale, after interviews shape the questions.
Research ✓
Census data on senior population size, income distribution, and car ownership rates in Cleveland and Cincinnati ZIP codes. Used to estimate market size before any customer contact.
Survey Design — Question Styles Used & Why
Likert scale (1–5) for price sensitivity: "How important is it to you that grocery delivery costs less than $20/month?" — Likert captures attitude without forcing a yes/no. We need to understand degree of price sensitivity, not just presence of it. Respondents who score 4–5 are our target. Below 3.5 average tells us the $18 price point is a barrier.
Open-ended for current behavior: "How do you currently get your groceries?" — We chose open-ended here because we don't want to constrain the answer. If a significant number say "my daughter comes every Saturday," that's a signal we didn't anticipate and could change our strategy entirely. Yes/No would miss it.
Demographic questions for segmentation: Age bracket, living situation (independent living facility vs. private home), and income range. This lets us identify which sub-segments are most likely to subscribe — and which ones may need a different pricing tier.
Data → Decision Flow
Step 1: Census research → market size estimate → if <3,000 seniors with limited mobility in target ZIP codes, do not proceed to customer interviews (market too small)
Step 2: Customer interviews → if >70% describe grocery access as a significant difficulty, proceed to survey. If <50%, flag market viability risk and present to QSS leadership before continuing.
Step 3: Survey Likert price data → if average score on price sensitivity is ≥3.5, current $18/month model is viable. If <3.5, prototype the lower-cost tier instead.
Step 4: Expert interviews → if senior services directors flag a competing service already operating in the market, assess whether differentiation is strong enough to proceed.

Cost-Driven vs. Value-Driven Diagnosis
Fresh Route is fundamentally cost-driven — their competitive advantage is affordability and reliability for a price-sensitive customer segment (fixed-income seniors). A value-driven model (premium packaging, concierge service, expanded product range) would miss the market entirely. This means QSS's recommendation must prioritize scale efficiencies: the business only becomes profitable when fixed costs are spread across enough subscribers. This is why market size matters so much in Phase 3.
Basic Cost-Benefit Analysis (base case, per new market)
Assumption: Average subscriber places 2.4 orders/month at $85 avg order value.
Revenue per subscriber/month: $18 subscription + (8% × $85 × 2.4) = $18 + $16.32 = $34.32
Variable cost per subscriber/month: 2.4 deliveries × $6.50 = $15.60
Contribution margin per subscriber: $34.32 − $15.60 = $18.72/month
Fixed cost coverage: $6,400 ÷ $18.72 = 342 subscribers needed to break even
Launch capital required: $85,000 (covers fixed costs through month 4 while building subscriber base)
Economies of Scale
Fixed costs don't grow proportionally with subscribers — a city manager can handle 200 or 800 subscribers at the same salary. Above 500 subscribers, the routing software subscription unlocks a bulk-route optimization feature that reduces average driver time per delivery by ~18%, dropping the variable cost from $6.50 to approximately $5.35 per delivery. This is the economies-of-scale inflection point. At 700 subscribers, this cost drop adds approximately $1,015/month in contribution margin — enough to fund the second city manager hire at ~60% of breakeven.

Recommendation
Proceed With Conditions
Financial reason: The base case requires 342 subscribers to break even by month 6, with a payback period of approximately 11 months on an $85K launch investment. That is a realistic but tight target. The risk is concentrated in two gates that QSS can verify before Fresh Route spends anything: (1) does the senior population in the target market have the problem, and (2) can a grocery partner be signed within the first 8 weeks? If both gates pass validation, the investment is justified. If either fails, the market should not be entered.
Condition 1 Complete Phase 3 validation before committing any launch capital — specifically, the customer interviews and market size check.
Condition 2 Launch Cleveland only first. Do not attempt both cities simultaneously — fixed costs would double before revenue has proven the model works in a new market.
Condition 3 Document the Columbus operations model before doing anything else. Without this, QSS has nothing to replicate.
What to take from this example
→Every section uses a specific class concept by name (Key Activities, 7Rs, Likert scale, fixed vs. variable costs). Not just the concept in general — applied to this specific client.
→Numbers are never dropped without an assumption. "$85K launch capital" comes from the fixed cost model, not from nowhere.
→The recommendation is specific enough to repeat without the slides: "342 subscribers to break even, 11-month payback, two validation gates before spending anything."
→Phases connect to each other — the failure points in Phase 1 (no grocery partner) become a gate in Phase 4. The assumptions in Phase 2 become the survey questions in Phase 3. Everything builds.
